40–60 MIN⌄
Block A · Strength
Pendulum / V-squat5×5⌄
Stay mid-foot between heels and balls, control the eccentric, hit full depth WITHOUT the pelvis tucking under — the machine's arc keeps the torso supported. Top set 4–6.
Swaps
Smith heel-elevated squat — busy-station; fixed path, heels up to spare ankle/back.
Heel-elevated goblet squat — minimal-equip; one DB, same quad bias, tall torso.
Leg press — disc-safer; back fully supported, zero axial load.
Watch for

Low back rounding / pelvic tuck (butt wink) at the bottom — stop depth before it happens; loaded lumbar flexion is the disc risk.

Knees caving in — track them over the 2nd/3rd toe.

A disc-injured back gets resilient from endurance and control, not a stronger low back. In APT the lumbar erectors are usually tight and overactive, not weak — directly "strengthening the low back" pulls you further into the arch. What bulletproofs it: core endurance + bracing, strong glutes and abs, hip/ankle mobility, and a grooved neutral-spine hinge. Brace, don't bend.

The Daily 5 — Big 3 + 2

~5–10 min, any day. Goal is endurance: short holds, smooth breathing, short rests, never grinding to failure. Use a descending pyramid (more sets early, fewer as you fatigue). Build holds and reps over weeks.

1 · Modified curl-up
One knee bent, other leg straight, hands under the low back to keep the natural arch (do NOT flatten). Lift head/shoulders ~1 inch — barely. The spine does not move, chin tucked. Dose: descending 6→4→2 reps, ~10s each.
2 · Side plank (side bridge)
Forearm under shoulder; knees bent (beginner) or legs straight stacked (advanced). Lift hips to a straight line, brace. No sagging or rotating. Dose: 5→3→1 reps/side, build toward 5×10s/side.
3 · Bird dog
Hands and knees, spine neutral, abs lightly braced. Extend opposite arm + leg to torso level — no higher. Hips/shoulders square — glass of water on the low back can't spill. Dose: 5–10/side, ~10s each.
4 · Glute bridge (ISO hold)
Knees bent, feet flat. Squeeze glutes, drive hips to a straight shoulder-to-knee line — do not arch the low back to get higher (glutes finish the rep). Dose: 3–5×10s, or 2×10 with a 2s squeeze.
5 · Couch / half-kneeling hip-flexor stretch
Half-kneeling, squeeze the down-leg glute and tuck the pelvis under (posterior tilt). The stretch comes from the glute squeeze + tuck, NOT from arching the back. Dose: 2×30–45s/side.

Bracing

  • 360° breath: inhale into belly, sides, and low back — expand all the way around the waistband.
  • Brace, then lift: stiffen abs like taking a light punch (~20–30% for daily lifts, harder for max). The brace makes the stiff cylinder that protects the disc. Exhale slowly through the sticking point.
  • Hinge cue: hips back, chest leads, spine neutral and locked — the bar moves because the hips move, not the spine.

Through the day

  • Stand ribs-over-pelvis, glutes lightly on; don't hang on one hip.
  • Break up sitting every 30–45 min; chair set so you're not slouched into flexion.
  • Morning back hygiene: discs are most swollen the first hour after waking — avoid toe-touches, deep bends, or heavy hinging first thing.

Stop doing

  • Loaded spinal flexion — no sit-ups, crunches, Russian twists, or rounding under load (the exact disc-herniation mechanism).
  • End-range loaded extension — no heavy back extensions / good mornings cranked into an arch.
  • Prolonged slouched sitting and ego-loading the hinge — leave reps in the tank, never let form round.

Progression

Extend hold time first (e.g. side plank 5×5s → 5×8s → 5×10s), then add reps, then advance the variation (knees-down → legs-straight side plank; bridge → single-leg or loaded hip-thrust ISO). Endurance goes up; spinal load does not. Signs it's working: longer pain-free holds, easier neutral hinge, less post-sitting stiffness, fewer flare-ups.

Red flags — stop and see a professional: sharp/shooting pain down the leg (sciatica) worsening or past the knee · numbness or pins-and-needles in leg, groin, or saddle area · foot/leg weakness, foot drop, a leg that gives out · any change in bladder or bowel control — emergency (possible cauda equina); go to the ER, don't wait.

The trained-core block lives inside the two lower days (Day 1, Day 4) and optionally one upper day (Day 5). It is distinct from the Daily 5 spine-hygiene flow. All movements are disc-safe: anti-extension, anti-rotation, anti-lateral-flexion — never loaded spinal flexion. NO sit-ups, crunches, or Russian twists.

Block A: isometric, lower reps, longer holds — anti-extension (hollow hold, reverse plank, dead bug) and anti-rotation (Pallof) for bracing endurance under tension.
Block B: more reps, more dynamic — add hanging leg raise and ab-wheel volume, longer/lighter carries, dynamic Pallof.

1 · Build the pull
Block A: weighted/assisted pull-ups 5×3–5 (full dead-hang to chin-over). Block B: heavier lat-pulldown volume 4×10–12 + ring rows to grow the horizontal and vertical pull together.

OPTIONAL⌄

Physique tuning

The dense, athletic look is shoulder-to-waist illusion + density at low body fat — not size. Three levers, in order:

  • Get lean. Diet is ~80% of this aesthetic. The "dense, hard" look is muscle you already have, made visible at ~12–14% body fat. Your fat-loss plan IS the physique plan — protein high, deficit steady.
  • Build width up top. Wide shoulders + lats taper the waist. Push HARD on cable lateral raises (12–20 reps, near failure, the bulk of shoulder volume), lat pulldown + pull-ups (lat width), and upper-back density via face pulls/rows. These create the V more than chest.
  • Density over pump-chasing. Keep compounds 5–10 reps for dense muscle; let isolations run 12–20. Train close to failure, progress the load. Don't chase a mass-monster — athletic + dense + lean wins.

Injury & mobility

The disc is the boss of this plan. Lumbar flexion under heavy compression is the riskiest position — so pin/block hinges (mid-shin start) keep the torso upright and cut shear, and skipping the back squat removes axial compression you don't need. Brace, don't bend — 360° brace, move at the HIPS, lumbar neutral, every hinge/carry/bridge. Endurance and stability beat max strength for keeping the back happy.

APT, honest version: you probably can't permanently re-angle the pelvis, and stretching hip flexors alone does little. What helps symptoms is strengthening glutes + abs and improving hip-flexor length together — bridges/thrusts, dead bugs/bird dogs, couch stretch with a posterior tilt. Train control, not a number in a side photo.

Ankle/hip: weight-bearing drills transfer — knee-to-wall dorsiflexion and ankle rocks (heel down) beat passive calf stretching; add banded mobilization if the joint, not the muscle, is the block. For hips, loaded full-range work (split squats, 90/90) builds usable mobility better than static stretching.

Grip & conditioning

The program hits all three grip types: crush (fat-grip work, hammer curls), support (farmer's/suitcase carries, dead hangs), pinch (plate pinch carries). Progress like any lift — add weight or distance/time when clean. Carries: heavy for short trips (3–4×20–30m) for strength, lighter/longer for conditioning. Don't grind every grip set to failure; tendons need a ramp.

Conditioning for fat loss without frying recovery: keep the hard stuff SHORT and crisp. Sled and heavy bag → short bouts, full-ish rest, quality over volume; they spare the joints/spine. Zone 2 (incline walk, bike) is the recovery-friendly engine — conversational, ~60–70% max HR. If conditioning leaves you too beat to progress your lifts, it's too much.

GBRS — what's worth stealing

GBRS Performance (Vernon Griffith II + DEVGRU vets) trains for durability and longevity, not peaking. Their own staples already dodge the two movements your disc can't tolerate. What's ported into this plan:

  • Loaded carry as the trunk/grip pillar — suitcase/front-rack carries replace dedicated core/grip work; anti-lateral-flexion + grip, no spinal loading. Their #1 transferable idea.
  • Conditioning as an end-of-session finisher, never the main event — sled/airbike, not running, to spare the back/ankle.
  • Trap-bar deadlift from blocks as the primary hinge — their signature pull, the most spine-neutral barbell hinge available.
  • Belt squat + Bulgarian split squat as the leg engine — zero spinal compression, mirrors GBRS leg day (they don't back squat either).
  • Mobility harvested INTO the lift, not bolted before it — the 3–4 highest-value drills (loaded ankle DF, 90/90, T-spine) live in the primer + rest periods, killing the 30-min mobility tax that made the old plan get quit.
  • Pain-vs-injury + train-around mindset and Zone 2 walking as the recovery default — Shipley's literal #1 recommendation.

Skipped on purpose: the standalone 30-min pre-lift mobility block, floor barbell deadlifts / any back squat, long rucks / loaded run volume, and the full 5-day split. Ideas ported, structure kept to your 4-lift + 1-conditioning + 2-recovery week.

RULES & PROGRESSION
Pain vs injury · deload · the physio note
READ ME⌄
Two non-negotiables: all hinging starts from rack pins/blocks at mid-shin, never the floor. No barbell back squat. No loaded spinal flexion, no end-range loaded extension. Both protect your disc.

Pain vs injury

A niggle means modify the movement, not cancel the session — only sharp, shooting, or numb signals mean stop. Learn the difference so you train around the small stuff instead of stopping for six months. If anything sends a sharp signal down your back or legs, stop and regress — never worth pushing through.

Progress within a block, jump between blocks

  • Week 1 establishes working loads. Week 2 adds one rep per set OR a small load bump at the same reps (double-progression).
  • When Block A comes back around, start heavier than the last A.
  • Leave 1–2 reps in the tank on every working set — never grind form into flexion.
  • Track waist + photos over the scale — holding strength while leaning out = winning.

Deload

Every 8 weeks (after 2 full A/B cycles): one easier week — keep the lifts, cut sets ~40%, drop load ~10%, keep movement quality high. Conditioning stays Zone 2 only that week.

The one physio visit

A confirmed herniated disc is worth one physio/PT assessment to confirm it's muscular and manageable vs. structural needing tailored care — and to check your directional preference. Not alarmist: one visit gives you a green light and a baseline. Most disc herniations improve with exactly this kind of loading-smart, endurance-based program. Bust the myths: bending isn't forbidden forever, "core" isn't endless crunches (it's bracing/anti-movement), and you don't need to fix a posture number to train hard.
