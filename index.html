The Daily 5
McGill Big 3 + 2 — your #1 priority
5–10 min⌄

Goal is endurance, not max effort. Short holds, smooth breathing, short rests — never grind to failure. Use a descending pyramid (more reps early, fewer as you fatigue). Build holds and reps over weeks; don't start at the top. Do it most days — lifting, recovery, or rest.

Modified curl-up
6→4→2 ×10s
⌄

One knee bent, other leg straight. Hands under the low back to keep its natural arch. Lift head + shoulders barely off the floor. Cue: the spine doesn't move — only head/chest lift, chin tucked.

▶ Watch how
WATCH FOR Don't flatten the low back into the floor (keep the natural arch), and no neck cranking — chin stays tucked.
Side plank
5→3→1 ×10s / side
⌄

Forearm under shoulder. Knees bent to start, legs straight to progress. Lift hips to a straight line, brace abs. Cue: straight line head-to-feet, hips up.

▶ Watch how
WATCH FOR No sagging at the hips and no rotating the torso — stay stacked and stiff.
Bird dog
5–10 ×10s / side
⌄

Hands and knees, spine neutral, abs lightly braced. Extend opposite arm + leg to level — no higher. Cue: hips and shoulders stay square — a glass of water on your low back can't spill.

▶ Watch how
WATCH FOR Hips/shoulders twisting or the back arching — move slow, keep the pelvis level.
Glute bridge (ISO hold)
3–5 ×10s
⌄

Knees bent, feet flat. Squeeze glutes and drive hips to a straight line shoulder-to-knee, hold at the top. Cue: the glutes finish the rep, not the spine.

▶ Watch how
WATCH FOR Don't arch the low back to get higher — finish in a posterior tilt, ribs down, glutes locked.
Couch / hip-flexor stretch
2 × 30–45s / side
⌄

Half-kneeling, rear shin on a bench/wall (or floor). Squeeze the down-leg glute and tuck the pelvis under. Cue: the stretch comes from the glute squeeze + pelvic tuck, not from arching the back.

▶ Watch how
WATCH FOR Arching the low back for "more stretch" — tuck the pelvis, squeeze the glute on the stretched side.
BRACE
Bracing & Daily Habits
Brace, don't bend
read once⌄

Brace before every working set:

  • 360° breath — inhale into belly and sides/low back, expand all the way around the waistband.
  • Brace, then lift — stiffen the abs like taking a light punch (~20–30% for daily loads, harder for max). Exhale slow through the sticking point.
  • Hinge cue — hips back, chest leads, spine neutral and locked. The bar moves because the hips move, not the spine.

Through the day:

  • Ribs stacked over pelvis — not flared ribs / butt pushed back. Glutes lightly on, don't hang on one hip.
  • Break up sitting — stand/walk every 30–45 min.
  • Morning hygiene — discs are swollen and flexion-vulnerable the first hour up; no toe-touches or heavy hinging first thing.

Stop doing:

  • No loaded spinal flexion — sit-ups, crunches, Russian twists, rounding under load (the exact mechanism that herniates discs).
  • No end-range loaded extension — heavy back extensions / good mornings cranked into an arch feed the tilt.
  • No ego-loading the hinge — leave reps in the tank; never round to move weight.
PROGRESS
Progression & Red Flags
How to build it — and when to stop
read once⌄

Progress the Big 3 over 4–8 weeks, in this order:

  • Extend the holds first — e.g. side plank 5×5s → 5×8s → 5×10s.
  • Then add reps — build curl-up / bird dog / side plank toward more reps at a clean 10s hold.
  • Then a harder variation — knees-down → legs-straight side plank; bridge → single-leg or loaded hip-thrust ISO. Endurance and control go up; spinal load doesn't.

Signs it's working: longer pain-free holds, easier neutral-spine hinge, less stiffness after sitting, fewer flare-ups, stronger carries/grip.

RED FLAGS — stop and get assessed:
· Sharp / shooting pain down the leg, worsening or past the knee
· Numbness or pins-and-needles in the leg, groin, or saddle area
· Foot or leg weakness, foot drop, a leg that gives out
· ANY change in bladder or bowel control = ER now (possible cauda equina — don't wait).

Not alarmist: a confirmed herniated disc is worth one physio visit to confirm it's muscular and manageable vs. structural, and to check your directional preference (most discs feel better with extension/standing, worse with flexion/sitting — which validates the avoid-flexion rules). One visit gives a green light and a baseline. Most herniations improve with exactly this loading-smart, endurance-based work.

Pull-up Plan · 2–3 → 10+

Pull-up Progression

Hit the target, move up a stage. A few easy reps on off days speeds this up.
Stage 1 — now
Max full reps first (e.g. 3,3,2,2), then finish sets with band/machine assist.
Stage 2 — negatives
Jump to the top, lower yourself slowly (3–5s). 4 × 3–4.
Stage 3 — assisted volume
Band or machine, full range, 4 × 6–8. Reduce help over time.
Stage 4 — weighted
Once you hit ~8 clean reps, add a little weight and keep going.

Go Deeper
PHYSIQUE
Physique Tuning
The shoulder-to-waist look
⌄

The look is shoulder-to-waist illusion + density at low body fat — not size. Three levers, in order of impact:

  • Get lean. Diet is ~80% of this aesthetic. The dense, hard look is muscle you already have, made visible at ~12–14% body fat. No training creates it under fat — your fat-loss plan IS the physique plan.
  • Build width up top. Wide shoulders + wide lats taper the waist. Push hard on cable lateral raises (12–20 reps, near failure), pulldowns + pull-ups for lat width, and upper-back density via face pulls/rows. These build the V more than chest does.
  • Density over pump-chasing. Keep compounds (bench, incline, rows, pull-ups) at 5–10 reps; let isolations (laterals, curls, kickbacks) run 12–20. Train close to failure, progress the load.

Bias the plan: add a lateral-raise set or two, never skip pull volume, keep the core tight. Don't chase mass-monster — that's the opposite look. Athletic + dense + lean wins. The mirror moves most when the deficit does.

The disc is the boss. Lumbar flexion under heavy compression is the riskiest position, so the hard rules hold: pin/block hinges (mid-shin start) keep the torso upright and cut spinal shear vs. the floor, and skipping the back squat removes axial compression you don't need. The cue that matters most: brace, don't bend — set a 360° core brace and move at the HIPS, lumbar neutral, on every hinge/carry/bridge. Endurance and stability beat max strength here (McGill's Big 3 — bird dog, side plank, modified curl-up — are in your recovery flow for exactly this).

APT: the honest version — you probably can't permanently re-angle the pelvis, and stretching hip flexors alone does little. What helps symptoms is strengthening glutes + abs while improving hip-flexor length: glute bridges/hip thrusts, dead bugs/bird dogs, and the couch stretch done with a posterior pelvic tilt (tuck or you just stretch the low back). Train control, not a number in a side photo.

Ankle/hip: weight-bearing drills transfer — knee-to-wall dorsiflexion and ankle rocks (heel down) beat passive calf stretching; add banded mobilization if the joint is the block. For hips, loaded full-range work (split squats, 90/90) builds usable mobility better than static stretching.

Do this: a herniated disc warrants one physio assessment to confirm whether your limit is muscular vs. structural — it changes what's safe to load. Bending isn't forbidden forever, "core" isn't endless crunches (it's bracing/anti-movement), and you don't need to fix a posture number to train hard.

CONDITIONING
Grip & Conditioning
Three grips, smart cardio
⌄

Grip — the program hits all three types:

  • Crush grip (closing the hand) — fat-grip work, hammer curls.
  • Support grip (holding a load) — farmer's and suitcase carries, dead hangs.
  • Pinch grip (thumb vs. fingers) — plate pinch carries.

Progress them like any lift: add weight or distance/time when the current load is clean. Carries — heavy for short trips (3–4 × 20–30m) for strength, lighter/longer for conditioning. Hangs and pinches — add seconds, then load. Don't grind every grip set to failure early; tendons need a ramp.

Conditioning for fat loss without frying recovery: keep the hard stuff SHORT and crisp. Sled intervals and heavy bag → short bouts, full-ish rest, quality over volume; they burn calories and spare the joints/spine (sled especially). Zone 2 (incline walk, bike) is the recovery-friendly engine — conversational pace, ~60–70% max HR, as long as you like. Rule of thumb: if conditioning leaves you too beat to progress your lifts, it's too much — pull back the intervals, keep the Zone 2.

Two non-negotiables: All hinging starts from rack pins/blocks, never the floor. No barbell back squat. Both protect your disc. If anything sends a sharp signal down your back or legs, stop and regress — that's never worth pushing through.

Pain vs injury: a niggle means modify the movement, not cancel the session — only sharp / shooting / numb signals mean stop.
Progress: add a rep or a little weight each session · keep 1–2 reps in the tank on main lifts.
Track: waist + photos beat the scale · holding strength while leaning out = winning.
Deload every 6–8 weeks (half the sets, keep it light).
