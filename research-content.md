# Bulletproof — Research Content

Evidence-grounded subs + form notes per exercise, then three cross-cutting writeups. Built around hard rules: hinges start from pins/blocks at mid-shin (never the floor), no barbell back squat, everything back-safe for a herniated lumbar disc.

---

## Per-Exercise

### Leg press

**Subs:**
- Hack squat machine — when press is busy; similar quad/glute load, back supported.
- Heel-elevated goblet squat — minimal-equipment day; one DB, same quad bias.
- Belt squat or single-leg press — disc-safer regression; unloads the spine entirely.

**Watch for:**
- Letting the lower back round and lift off the pad at the bottom — stop the depth before the pelvis tucks (lumbar flexion under load is the disc risk).
- Locking knees hard at the top — keep a soft knee, don't slam the joint.

### Heel-elevated goblet squat

**Subs:**
- Leg press — when you want more load without holding a DB at the chest.
- Hack squat — when grip/upper back fatigue is the limiter, not the legs.
- Box squat to a high box — disc-safer regression; caps depth so the pelvis can't tuck.

**Watch for:**
- Pelvic tuck ("butt wink") at the bottom — the heel elevation should prevent it; if it still happens, raise the heels more or cut depth.
- Elbows drifting down and chest collapsing forward — keep the torso tall, ribs stacked over hips.

### Hip thrust

**Subs:**
- Single-leg hip thrust — when the bench/barbell setup is taken; bodyweight or one DB.
- Glute bridge off the floor — minimal-equipment option, shorter ROM.
- Cable pull-through or machine glute drive — disc-safer; loads glutes with less spinal compression.

**Watch for:**
- Hyperextending the low back at the top to chase height — full HIP extension is not spinal extension; ribs flaring + back bowing means the erectors took over and the spine is being compressed (EMG shows an erector spike at lockout tied to back pain). Finish with a posterior pelvic tilt, ribs down.
- Chin/eyes up to the ceiling — keep a slight chin tuck and look forward/down so the lumbar doesn't over-arch.

### Leg extension

**Subs:**
- Sissy squat (assisted) — when the machine is busy; bodyweight quad isolation.
- Heel-elevated goblet squat — compound alternative that still hammers quads.
- Single-leg extension — even out side-to-side; same machine, lighter regression.

**Watch for:**
- Yanking with momentum and slamming into lockout — control the eccentric, pause briefly at the top.
- Butt sliding off the seat / arching to move more weight — keep the back pinned to the pad.

### Standing calf raise

**Subs:**
- Seated calf raise — when standing station is busy; biases soleus, spine unloaded.
- Leg-press calf press — disc-safer; no axial load on the spine at all.
- Single-leg bodyweight raise off a step — minimal-equipment / different gym.

**Watch for:**
- Bouncing through partial ROM — get a full stretch at the bottom and a full squeeze at the top, pause both ends.
- For the disc: prefer seated or leg-press calf work over heavy standing/loaded-on-shoulders versions to keep axial compression off the spine.

### Heavy farmer's carry

**Subs:**
- Trap-bar carry — when DBs/handles are taken; easier to load heavy, neutral grip.
- Single-arm (suitcase) carry — minimal-equipment; also trains anti-lateral-flexion core.
- Loaded sled drag (forward) — disc-safer regression; grip + legs with near-zero spinal load.

**Watch for:**
- Leaning/shrugging up into the traps and losing a neutral spine — stand tall, ribs stacked, brace before you pick up.
- Grabbing from the floor with a rounded back — set the load on blocks/pins at mid-shin height to start (hard rule), or use a trap bar with elevated handles.

### Sled push (Day 1 finisher)

**Subs:**
- Prowler/heavy sled — interchangeable if your gym labels them differently.
- Incline treadmill push-walk (hands on rail, high incline) — when no sled; similar leg drive, conditioning.
- Bike or stair sprint intervals — minimal-equipment finisher.

**Watch for:**
- Rounding the back to push low handles — keep a long spine, drive through the legs, lean from the hips not the lumbar.
- Going so heavy form breaks down — a finisher should tax the legs/lungs, not turn into a grind that lights up the back.

### Barbell bench press

**Subs:**
- Incline DB press — when the rack/bench is busy; also shifts emphasis to upper chest.
- Machine chest press / Smith bench — disc-safer regression; back supported, easy to bail solo.
- Flat DB press — minimal-equipment; more stretch, easier on the shoulders.

**Watch for:**
- Flaring elbows to 90° and pressing with shoulders forward — tuck elbows ~45–60°, retract/pin the shoulder blades to protect the joint.
- Massive lower-back bridge to shorten the press — a small natural arch is fine, but don't let the lumbar overextend; keep glutes on the bench and feet planted.

### Incline DB press

**Subs:**
- Incline machine / Smith incline — when DBs are taken; easier to overload safely.
- Flat or low-incline DB press — minimal-equipment; same pattern, different emphasis.
- Incline cable press — constant tension option, joint-friendly.

**Watch for:**
- Setting the bench too steep (>45°) — turns it into a shoulder press and steals upper-chest work; keep it ~30°.
- Letting elbows flare wide at the bottom — keep a slight tuck to protect the shoulder.

### Standing DB shoulder press

**Subs:**
- Seated DB press (back supported) — disc-safer regression; removes the standing spinal load.
- Machine shoulder press — when DBs are busy; fixed path, easy to load.
- Landmine press — back-friendly pressing angle, less overhead strain.

**Watch for:**
- Leaning back into a standing-incline-bench position and arching the lumbar to grind reps — brace the core, ribs down; if the low back arches to finish, the weight's too heavy. (Given the disc, default to seated/supported.)
- Pressing with elbows way forward or flaring hard — keep them slightly in front of the torso, stack wrists over elbows.

### Ring dips

**Subs:**
- Parallel-bar / machine dips — when rings are taken or for more stability.
- Bench/box dips (feet forward) — easier regression, scalable.
- Close-grip / decline push-ups — minimal-equipment substitute.

**Watch for:**
- Dropping too deep with shoulders rolling forward past a comfortable stretch — keep depth to where the shoulder stays stable, don't sink into pain.
- Flaring elbows wide and shrugging up — keep elbows tracking back, shoulders down/packed.

### Cable lateral raise

**Subs:**
- DB lateral raise — when the cable is busy; peak tension at top instead of through the range.
- Lateral-raise machine — fixed path, easy to push near failure.
- Lean-away single-arm cable raise — best stretch on the side delt; do one arm at a time.

**Watch for:**
- Swinging/heaving with the traps and using momentum — go lighter, lead with the elbow, control the lowering.
- Shrugging the shoulder up toward the ear — keep the neck long, drive width out to the side not up.

### Triceps pushdown

**Subs:**
- Overhead cable/rope extension — when the station is busy; biases the long head (more stretch).
- DB or EZ-bar skull crusher — minimal-equipment; keep it light to protect elbows.
- Ring/bench dips — compound triceps option.

**Watch for:**
- Elbows drifting forward and the shoulders/lats doing the work — pin elbows to the sides, only the forearm moves.
- Leaning the whole torso into it to push more weight — stay upright, let the triceps drive it.

### Fat-grip dead hang

**Subs:**
- Towel-over-bar hang — when no fat grips; same support-grip overload.
- Standard bar hang + Fat Gripz on a cable handle — minimal-equipment swap.
- Fat-grip carry hold (standing) — back-friendlier if shoulder hang is uncomfortable.

**Watch for:**
- Hanging totally passive with shoulders at the ears the entire time — fine briefly, but add a slight scapular "pack" (shoulders down) for a healthier shoulder position.
- Going to full hand failure every set early on — grip and pulleys/tendons need ramp-up; build hang time progressively.

### Sled intervals (Day 3)

**Subs:**
- Prowler pushes/drags — same stimulus, whatever's available.
- Incline treadmill walk-pushes or bike sprints — when no sled.
- Stair intervals — minimal-equipment conditioning.

**Watch for:**
- Sprinting so hard form collapses and the back rounds on heavy pushes — keep a long spine, moderate the load for intervals.
- Too much volume that wrecks recovery for the lifting days — keep intervals short and crisp (see Grip & Conditioning).

### Heavy bag (Day 3)

**Subs:**
- Shadowboxing with light DBs — when no bag; keeps it conditioning-focused.
- Battle ropes — similar full-body interval stimulus, zero spinal load.
- Jump rope / fast sled — alternate conditioning modes.

**Watch for:**
- Wild rotational power punches that torque the lumbar — for the disc, keep rotation controlled and core-braced; favor volume/pace over max-power hooks.
- Wrist/hand injury from poor wrapping or bad fist — wrap up, snap punches, don't muscle into the bag.

### Zone 2 run (Day 3)

**Subs:**
- Incline treadmill walk — disc/joint-safer; same Zone 2 aerobic stimulus, less impact.
- Bike or elliptical Zone 2 — minimal-impact, easy on ankles/back.
- Rower at easy pace — full-body Zone 2 (watch lumbar position).

**Watch for:**
- Drifting too hard out of true Zone 2 (should be conversational, ~60–70% max HR) — going harder turns recovery cardio into a stressor.
- Overstriding/heel-slamming with weak ankles — shorten the stride, or swap to incline walk if ankle mobility makes running ugly.

### Plate pinch carries (Day 3 add-on)

**Subs:**
- Two smooth plates pinched per hand — standard setup; scale by plate weight/count.
- Hub/grip-block pinch hold — when no suitable plates; static pinch.
- Towel pinch carry — minimal-equipment pinch alternative.

**Watch for:**
- Letting plates slowly slip and catching with a jerky regrip — pick a weight you can hold cleanly for the full distance.
- Hunching/shrugging under the load — stand tall, brace; this trains pinch grip, not your back.

### RDL from pins/blocks

**Subs:**
- Trap-bar RDL/deadlift from elevated handles — when the rack is busy; more upright torso, less shear on the spine.
- Single-leg or B-stance RDL (DBs from a low step) — disc-safer regression; lighter spinal load, balance/glute focus.
- Lying/seated hamstring curl — when the back's cranky; hamstring work with zero spinal load.

**Watch for:**
- Treating it like a deadlift and lifting with the back instead of hinging — push the hips BACK toward the wall, soft knees, shins vertical; the bar should drag up the legs. (Hard rule: start from pins/blocks at mid-shin, never the floor.)
- Over-rounding OR over-arching the lumbar at the bottom — keep a braced neutral spine; stop the range when the hamstrings are stretched, before the back position changes. Erectors lighting up = range too long or load too heavy.

### Lying/seated hamstring curl

**Subs:**
- Swap lying↔seated — whichever machine is open; seated adds hip-flexed stretch.
- Nordic curl (assisted) / sliding leg curl — minimal-equipment; heavy eccentric.
- Single-leg curl — fix side-to-side imbalance; same machine.

**Watch for:**
- Hips popping up off the pad to cheat the weight up — keep hips pinned; if they rise, lighten the load.
- Slamming the weight back down — control the eccentric, that's where most of the growth is.

### Bulgarian split squat

**Subs:**
- Walking or reverse lunges — when no bench; reverse lunge is easiest on the knee.
- Split squat (both feet on floor) — easier regression, more stable, less balance demand.
- Leg press / hack squat — disc-and-balance-safer when fatigued or back is cranky.

**Watch for:**
- Heel of the front foot lifting (from limited ankle dorsiflexion) — push through the whole foot; if the heel rises, step the front foot farther out or work ankle mobility. Heel-up loads the knee.
- Sitting too upright and over-arching the lower back into APT — lean the torso ~25–35° forward over the front leg to keep a neutral spine and load the glute/hamstring.

### 45° back extension

**Subs:**
- Glute-focused 45° hyper (round the upper back slightly, posterior tilt) — same station, glute bias.
- Cable pull-through — when the bench is taken; hip-hinge pattern, glute/hamstring, low spinal load.
- Bird dog / reverse hyper (light) — disc-safer regression; trains the pattern without heavy spinal extension.

**Watch for:**
- Cranking into lumbar HYPER-extension at the top — rise only to a straight line (neutral), don't arch past it; over-extension is a disc/facet irritant. Hinge from the hips, not the low back.
- Adding heavy load too soon — for a herniated disc, start bodyweight, master a neutral finish, then progress slowly.

### Cable glute kickback

**Subs:**
- Machine glute kickback / hip extension — when the cable's busy; same isolation.
- Banded standing kickback — minimal-equipment / different gym.
- Quadruped bird-dog hip extension — disc-safer bodyweight version.

**Watch for:**
- Arching the low back to "throw" the leg higher — the extra range comes from the lumbar, not the glute; keep the torso still and core braced, move only at the hip.
- Rushing reps with momentum — squeeze the glute at the top, control the return.

### Suitcase carry

**Subs:**
- Single-arm DB/kettlebell carry — same thing, whatever load's free.
- Single-arm farmer's hold (static) — minimal space; isometric anti-lateral-flexion.
- Side plank — disc-aware regression that trains the same anti-side-bend core (McGill staple).

**Watch for:**
- Letting the torso bend toward the weighted side — stay perfectly upright; the point is resisting the lean (anti-lateral-flexion core + grip).
- Rounding to set the bell down — hinge from pins/blocks height or squat down with a neutral spine to place it.

### Backward sled drag (Day 4 finisher)

**Subs:**
- Forward sled drag — when you want more posterior-chain/conditioning.
- Reverse incline-treadmill walk (holding rails) — when no sled; similar knee-friendly quad work.
- Leg extension burnout — minimal-equipment quad finisher.

**Watch for:**
- Hunching over the straps — stay fairly upright, let the legs do the work; this is knee-friendly quad/conditioning, keep the back neutral.
- Treating a finisher like a max effort — moderate load, keep moving.

### Pull-up progression

**Subs:**
- Assisted pull-up machine / band-assisted — when building from 2–3 reps; the main progression tool.
- Lat pulldown (neutral or wide) — when the bar's busy; scalable vertical pull.
- Ring rows / inverted rows — easier horizontal regression that still builds the pull.

**Watch for:**
- Kipping/swinging and half-reps — control the full range, dead hang to chin-over; partials stall progress.
- Shrugging up with shoulders at the ears — start each rep by depressing the shoulder blades (pack the lats) before pulling.

### Chest-supported row

**Subs:**
- Machine row (chest pad) — when the bench is taken; same back-safe support.
- Seated cable row — disc-aware alternative; keep the torso still, no jerking.
- Single-arm DB row (hand on bench) — minimal-equipment; supported, neutral spine.

**Watch for:**
- Heaving with the whole body and using the lower back — the chest pad exists so the spine stays neutral; if you're rocking, lighten it.
- Pulling with the arms only / not retracting — drive the elbows back and squeeze the shoulder blades together.

### Ring row

**Subs:**
- Inverted row on a bar/Smith — when rings are taken; same horizontal pull.
- Chest-supported / seated cable row — easier to load and keep strict.
- TRX row — interchangeable suspension option.

**Watch for:**
- Hips sagging and the body bending at the waist — keep a straight plank line from heels to head, glutes/core braced (protects the low back too).
- Half-pulling — bring the chest to the rings, elbows back, full squeeze.

### Neutral-grip lat pulldown

**Subs:**
- Wide-grip pulldown — when you want more width emphasis; neutral is just easier on shoulders/elbows.
- Assisted pull-up — when the cable's busy; vertical pull, builds toward bodyweight.
- Single-arm cable pulldown — fix imbalances, bigger stretch.

**Watch for:**
- Leaning way back and turning it into a row — stay fairly upright (slight lean is fine), pull to the upper chest.
- Yanking with momentum and not controlling the eccentric — let the lats stretch fully at the top under control.

### Face pull / rear-delt fly

**Subs:**
- Reverse pec-deck — when the cable's busy; same rear-delt/upper-back target.
- Band pull-apart / band face pull — minimal-equipment; great for posture/shoulder health.
- Bent-over DB rear fly (chest supported on incline bench) — keep it supported to spare the back.

**Watch for:**
- Using too much weight and rowing with the lats instead of the rear delts — go light, lead with the elbows high, pull toward the forehead/eyes.
- Shrugging the traps up — keep shoulders down, think "spread the rope."

### DB or barbell curl

**Subs:**
- Cable curl — when DBs/bar are taken; constant tension.
- Incline DB curl — minimal-equipment; bigger biceps stretch.
- Machine/preacher curl — strict regression, less cheating.

**Watch for:**
- Swinging the torso / using the lower back to heave the weight up — stand braced, or sit; if you're leaning back, it's too heavy (and it loads the spine).
- Elbows drifting forward into a partial rep — keep elbows pinned at the sides, full range.

### Fat-grip hammer curl

**Subs:**
- Rope hammer curl on cable — when no fat grips; neutral grip, constant tension.
- Fat Gripz on DBs or towel-wrapped handle — minimal-equipment fat-grip option.
- Standard hammer curl — drop the fat grip if forearm/grip fatigue limits the biceps work.

**Watch for:**
- Letting grip fatigue end the set before the biceps/brachialis are worked — that's partly the point (grip), but if growth is the goal, alternate fat-grip and normal-grip sets.
- Swinging to compensate for the harder grip — keep it strict, lighten if needed.

### Couch stretch (Recovery)

**Subs:**
- Half-kneeling hip-flexor stretch (rear foot on floor) — easier regression if the knee/quad stretch is too intense.
- Standing hip-flexor lunge stretch — minimal-space option.
- Kneeling hip-flexor with posterior pelvic tilt — best for APT (tilt the pelvis under to actually stretch the hip flexor, not the low back).

**Watch for:**
- Arching the lower back to feel "more stretch" — that's the lumbar moving, not the hip flexor; tuck the pelvis under (posterior tilt) and squeeze the glute on the stretched side.
- Cranking into pain — mobility work should be a moderate stretch, not max-effort.

### 90/90 hip switches (Recovery)

**Subs:**
- Seated 90/90 hold (no switch) — easier regression; just sit tall in each position.
- Half-kneeling hip rotations — minimal-space option.
- Lying windshield wipers (controlled) — floor-based hip rotation.

**Watch for:**
- Rounding the back to get lower — sit tall, hinge from the hips; let the range come from the hips, not lumbar flexion.
- Forcing range and bouncing — move slowly through positions, own each one.

### Ankle dorsiflexion rocks (Recovery)

**Subs:**
- Knee-to-wall dorsiflexion drill — best version; measurable, weight-bearing (the kind that transfers).
- Half-kneeling ankle rock — minimal-space; load the front shin over the toes.
- Banded ankle mobilization (band pulling the ankle forward) — adds joint glide for stubborn ankles.

**Watch for:**
- Letting the heel pop off the floor — keep the heel DOWN; that's the whole point of weight-bearing dorsiflexion.
- Knee caving inward — track the knee over the 2nd/3rd toe.

### Dead bug + bird dog (Recovery)

**Subs:**
- Dead bug alone (feet supported) — easier regression; learn the brace first.
- Bird dog alone — minimal-equipment anti-extension/anti-rotation core (McGill Big 3 staple).
- Side plank — rounds out the McGill core trio for the disc.

**Watch for:**
- Letting the lower back arch off the floor on dead bugs — press the low back into the floor and brace before the limb moves (this is the anti-extension skill that protects the disc).
- Hips/shoulders twisting on bird dogs — move slow, keep the pelvis level; reach long, don't crank into lumbar extension.

### Bodyweight glute bridge (Recovery)

**Subs:**
- Single-leg glute bridge — harder progression; more glute, fixes imbalance.
- Hip thrust (light) — loaded version of the same pattern.
- Banded glute bridge — adds tension with minimal equipment.

**Watch for:**
- Pushing the hips up by arching the lower back instead of squeezing the glutes — finish in a posterior tilt (ribs down, glutes locked), don't hyperextend the lumbar.
- Feeling it in the hamstrings/low back, not the glutes — move the feet slightly closer and drive through the heels.

### Cat-cow + open book (Recovery)

**Subs:**
- Cat-cow alone — gentle spinal mobility regression.
- Open book alone — thoracic rotation focus.
- Seated thoracic rotations — minimal-space option.

**Watch for:**
- Forcing end-range flexion/extension aggressively given the disc — keep cat-cow gentle and pain-free; this is mobility, not a stretch to push into.
- Rotating from the lumbar on open books — let the rotation come from the upper back (thoracic), keep the hips/low back quiet.

### Passive bar hang (Recovery)

**Subs:**
- Assisted/feet-supported hang — regression if full bodyweight is too much for grip/shoulders.
- Towel or fat-grip hang — doubles as grip work.
- Doorway/rings hang — minimal-equipment option.

**Watch for:**
- Hanging fully limp with zero shoulder awareness every time — passive is the goal for decompression, but mix in a light scapular pack for shoulder health.
- Overdoing duration early — build hang time gradually so grip and shoulders adapt.

## Physique Tuning

The Memento/AHX look is **shoulder-to-waist illusion + density at low body fat** — not size. Three levers, in order of impact:

1. **Get lean.** Diet is ~80% of this aesthetic. That "dense, blocky, hard" look is just muscle you already have made visible at ~12–14% body fat. No amount of training creates it under a layer of fat. Your fat-loss plan IS the physique plan — protein high, deficit steady.
2. **Build width up top.** Wide shoulders + wide lats taper the waist. Prioritize and push HARD on: **cable lateral raises** (medial delts should be the bulk of shoulder volume — go higher frequency, 12–20 reps, near failure), **lat pulldown + pull-ups** (lat width), and upper-back density via **face pulls / rows**. These create the V more than chest does.
3. **Density over pump-chasing.** Keep compounds (bench, incline, rows, pull-ups) in the 5–10 rep range for dense, hard muscle; let isolations (laterals, curls, kickbacks) run 12–20. Train close to failure, progress the load.

Bias the plan: add a lateral-raise set or two, never skip the pull volume, keep core tight (a controlled waist sharpens the taper). Don't chase a "mass-monster" — that's the opposite look. Athletic + dense + lean wins. The mirror moves most when the scale and the deficit do.

## Injury & Mobility

**The disc is the boss of this plan.** Lumbar flexion under heavy compression is the riskiest position for a herniated disc, so the hard rules are correct: pin/block hinges (mid-shin start) keep the torso more upright and cut spinal shear vs. pulling from the floor, and skipping the barbell back squat removes a heavy axial-compression movement you don't need. The cue that matters most: **brace, don't bend** — set a 360° core brace and move at the HIPS, keeping the lumbar in neutral, on every hinge/carry/bridge. Endurance and stability beat max strength for keeping the back happy (McGill's "Big 3" — bird dog, side plank, modified curl-up — are in your recovery flow for exactly this).

**APT:** the honest version — you probably can't permanently re-angle the pelvis, and stretching hip flexors alone does little (research shows a non-significant change). What helps symptoms is **strengthening glutes + abs and improving hip-flexor length together**: glute bridges/hip thrusts, dead bugs/bird dogs, and the couch stretch done with a posterior pelvic tilt (tuck the pelvis or you just stretch the low back). Train control, not a number in a side photo.

**Ankle/hip:** weight-bearing drills transfer — **knee-to-wall dorsiflexion** and ankle rocks (heel down) beat passive calf stretching; add banded mobilization if the joint, not the muscle, is the block. For hips, loaded full-range work (split squats, 90/90) builds usable mobility better than endless static stretching.

**Caveat (do this):** a herniated disc warrants **one physio assessment** to confirm whether your limitation is muscular vs. structural — it changes what's safe to load. Bust the myths: bending isn't forbidden forever, "core" isn't endless crunches (it's bracing/anti-movement), and you don't need to "fix" a posture number to train hard and feel good.

## Grip & Conditioning

**Grip:** the program hits all three grip types. **Crush grip** (closing the hand) — fat-grip work and hammer curls. **Support grip** (holding a heavy load) — farmer's and suitcase carries, dead hangs. **Pinch grip** (thumb vs. fingers) — plate pinch carries. Progress them like any lift: add weight or distance/time when the current load is clean. Carries — go heavy for short trips (3–4 × 20–30m) for strength; lighter/longer for conditioning. Hangs and pinches — add seconds, then load. Don't grind every grip set to failure early; tendons need a ramp.

**Conditioning for fat loss without frying recovery:** keep the hard stuff SHORT and crisp. Sled intervals and heavy bag → short bouts, full-ish rest, quality over volume — they burn calories and spare the joints/spine (sled especially). Zone 2 (incline walk, bike) is the recovery-friendly engine work: conversational pace, ~60–70% max HR, as long as you like — it adds fat-loss calories without taxing the legs you need for lifting. Rule of thumb: if conditioning leaves you too beat to progress your lifts, it's too much — pull back the intervals, keep the Zone 2.
